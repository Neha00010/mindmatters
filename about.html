PHOBIA

An overwhelming and debilitating fear of an object, place, situation, feeling or animal.

This unjustified and excessive worry can cause serious distress and negatively impact daily living. Fears that people frequently experience include those of heights, spiders, small places, flying, and public speaking, among others. Numerous factors, such as heredity, previous experiences, and learnt behaviours, might contribute to the development of phobias. Depending on the severity of the fear and the needs of the individual, phobia treatment may involve counselling, such as cognitive-behavioral therapy, exposure therapy, or medication.

DEPRESSION

A bruise in your mind.

Depression is a frequent mental health condition marked by enduring melancholy, hopelessness, and a loss of interest or pleasure in once-enjoyable activities. Changes in sleeping or eating patterns, exhaustion, trouble focusing, feelings of shame or unworthiness, and persistent thoughts of suicide or death are some other signs of depression. Depression can be caused by a combination of biological, environmental, and psychological factors. Some of the known risk factors for depression include a family history of depression, chronic stress or trauma, and certain medical conditions or medications.

ANXIETY

Anxiety is a lot like a toddler.

Anxiety is a normal reaction to stress that includes uneasiness, concern, and apprehension about a possible threat or danger in the future. When faced with challenging circumstances, anxiety can be a healthy and beneficial response that keeps us awake and focused. However, anxiety can interfere with daily life and develop into a mental health issue when it is overwhelming or persistent. A set of mental health illnesses known as anxiety disorders are characterised by acute and enduring emotions of anxiety and fear. These disorders include panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, particular phobias, generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), and others.

PTSD

You survived the abuse.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, or PTSD, is a mental health issue that some persons who have encountered or seen a horrific event may develop. It can happen as a result of exposure to situations like natural catastrophes, major accidents, physical or sexual assault, war or conflict, or other potentially fatal situations. A variety of symptoms, such as intrusive and upsetting memories of the traumatic event, nightmares, flashbacks, intense distress when reminded of the event, avoiding triggers connected to the event, negative thoughts or feelings, and hyperarousal (such as irritability, hypervigilance, and trouble falling asleep) can all be signs of PTSD.

BORDER PERSONALITY DISORDER

Living with BPD is pure confusion.

Borderline personality disorder is known as BPD. A widespread pattern of instability in mood, behaviour, self-image, and relationships defines BPD as a mental health condition. Intense emotions, trouble controlling emotions, a fear of abandonment, impulsivity, and shaky relationships are some of the difficulties that people with BPD may experience. BPD is often treated with a combination of treatment and medication, which can be helpful in symptom management and overall life quality improvement.BPD is often diagnosed in young adulthood, and can be challenging to treat due to its complex and multifaceted nature.

OBSESSIVE-COMPLUSIVE DISORDER

Habit and routine have an unbelievable power to destroy.

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a mental health condition characterised by recurrent and persistent intrusive thoughts, images, or impulses that cause anxiety, as well as repetitive behaviours or mental acts that people feel compelled to perform in response to these obsessions. Obsessions that are common include the demand for symmetry or exactness, worries about contamination or cleanliness, and fear of harm to oneself or others. Compulsions might involve repetitive actions like counting, repeatedly checking locks, or obsessive hand washing. OCD can significantly impair daily functioning and cause significant distress.
